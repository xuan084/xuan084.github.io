This week we covered continuous distributions — uniform, exponential, normal — the kind of material that lives or dies on whether students can sit with the idea that probability now means *area under a curve* instead of a count. As a tutor for QBUS5001 *Foundation in Data Analysis for Business* at the University of Sydney, I get to spend two hours a week on exactly this kind of conceptual handoff.

I had forgotten how much I love it.

<figure class="post-photo">
  <img src="/images/2025-05-16-usyd-teaching/1.jpg" alt="Tutorial room at USYD">
  <figcaption>A quiet moment between problems.</figcaption>
</figure>

Preparing a tutorial is its own small craft: deciding which examples to lead with, which intuitions to build before the formulas land, which questions to leave hanging long enough that someone in the back gets there on their own. The version of me that learned these distributions years ago was patient with herself in a way that the rushing PhD version sometimes isn't — and stepping back into that pace, slowly, deliberately, on purpose, has been quietly grounding.

There is a particular moment in a tutorial — you can usually see it in someone's face — where a concept that was words a few minutes ago becomes *the same thing said differently*. With continuous distributions it often happens at the integral. *Oh.* And then they're off, asking the next question, the one that means they've moved past the fear and into the real curiosity.

What surprised me most this week wasn't that I enjoyed teaching, but that I felt genuinely *useful*. Research has its own slow joys, but most of what I do day-to-day is uncertain — running an experiment that might not work, drafting paragraphs that get rewritten three times, sitting with a problem that may not have a solution this month or next. Teaching is the opposite kind of work. A student walks in not understanding a thing, and ninety minutes later, with my help, they do. The signal is fast, and it's clean.

I came home tired and a little hoarse, but with that very particular feeling: *something useful happened today.* It's a feeling I want to keep showing up for.
